The State of Privacy

Your cell phone helps you keep in touch with friends and family, but it’s also a collection of:
where you've been,
who you talk to,
what you say,
and everything you do online.

Here are some things to consider:

Read More

4.5 million smartphones were lost or stolen in the United States in 2013, or 4% of all active smartphones.

That's equivalent to the populations of Colorado, Kansas, Nebraska, Wyoming, and Utah combined as compared to the United States.

21% of internet users have had an email or social networking account compromised or taken over by someone else without permission.

Thats equivalent to the populations of Arizona, Nevada, Texas, Idaho, Montana, Oregon, Washington, Oklahoma, North Dakota, South Dakota, and Louisiana combined as compared to the United States.

34% of smartphone users have no active security measures like passwords or lock screens.

That's equivalent to the populations of Florida, Mississippi, Georgia, and Alabama as compared to the United States.

68% of internet users believe that current laws are not good enough in protecting people’s privacy online...

Comparable to: population of U.S. with a smartphone.

... and with Congress still having no idea how many Americans have been spied on by NSA's mass surveillance programs, it's safe to presume 100% of internet users are at risk.

Here’s what you can do, now.

Help protect the privacy of yourself and the people you communicate with while also resisting bulk surveillance systems by following these steps.

Remember: even if you feel you have nothing to hide, you have the right to be free from unreasonable search and seizure.

Password lock your phone, now.

Your passwords should be a long mix of capital and lowercase letters, numbers and punctuation symbols.

Remember: You do not have to give your encryption keys or passwords to law enforcement. Know your rights.

Enable Two-Factor Authentication, now.

This is a security process that requires two levels of identification, it can help keep your information safe even if someone has your password.

Gmail, Google services
Apple ID
Microsoft Accounts
Yahoo! Mail
Facebook, called “login approvals”
Twitter, called “login verification”
A growing list of services offering two-factor authentication.

When it comes to app privileges, use your common sense:
 
When not using features like GPS, bluetooth, WiFi, you should disable them.
When installing a new app, ask yourself “why does this app need these permissions?”
Disable any app permissions and location services that you feel are unnecessary.
